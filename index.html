Jeju Island Haenyeo

Women Divers

The Jeju Haenyeo, or "sea women," are female free divers from Jeju Island in South Korea, known for their unique tradition of diving to harvest seafood without modern diving equipment, a practice with roots stretching back centuries.

Historical Context and Evolution:

    Origins
  • While free diving in Jeju dates back to 430 AD, women became the dominant force in the 18th century, taking over from men who were away at war or fishing.

    • Necessity and Tradition:
  • It's believed that Jeju women took up diving to support their families, with men often away at sea or in the military.

    • Semi-matriarchal Society
  • The Haenyeo culture is seen as representative of the semi-matriarchal family structure of Jeju Island.


    • What They Do

    Free Diving
  • The Haenyeo dive into the ocean, often to depths of 20 meters, to collect shellfish, seaweed, sea urchins, and other seafood.

    • Skills and Techniques
  • They are highly skilled free divers, known for their ability to hold their breath for extended periods and their knowledge of the marine environment.

    • Unmatched Skills
  • Some reports mention they can hold their breath for up to 3 minutes underwater and stay underwater for up to 20 minutes.

    • Unique Culture
  • The Haenyeo have a unique way of diving, using whistles and calls to communicate, and a floating net to collect the seafood.

    • Prayers and Traditions
  • Before a dive, prayers are said to the Jamsugut, the goddess of the sea, for safety and an abundant catch.

    • Group Dynamics
  • Divers are categorized into groups based on experience (hagun, junggun, and sanggun), with the sanggun offering guidance to the others

    • Teaching and Passing Down Traditions
  • Knowledge about diving and sea life is passed down through families, schools, local fishery cooperatives, and Haenyeo associations.


    • Current Status and Challenges

    Declining Numbers
  • While the Haenyeo tradition is a vibrant cultural heritage, it's facing challenges, with younger generations less inclined to follow in the footsteps of their elders.

    • UNESCO Recognition
  • In 2016, the culture of the Jeju Haenyeo was included on the UNESCO List of Intangible Cultural Heritage.

    • A Legacy of Resilience
  • The Haenyeo represent the resilience and strength of women in the face of challenging circumstances, as well as a unique cultural tradition.

    • Ongoing Efforts
  • Organizations like the Haenyeo Association and Haenyeo Museum, are actively involved in preserving and promoting the Haenyeo culture and way of life.
